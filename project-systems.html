Orion Design System

A design system built from scratch - a single source of truth for a product team that had accumulated years of design debt and inconsistency. Orion wasn't just a component library; it was a product for designers and developers.

Overview

The Starting Point

The product had three years of accumulated design decisions with no coherent system behind them. Colours existed in multiple nearly-identical variants. Spacing was arbitrary. Components were rebuilt by each designer from scratch. Design-to-dev handoff took days of back-and-forth.

The real cost wasn't the inconsistency you could see - it was the cognitive load on every designer, every sprint. The system was the problem.

Architecture

How It's Built

Orion follows atomic design: atoms, molecules, organisms, templates. But the architecture that matters most is the token layer - colour, typography, spacing, radius, and motion tokens that cascade from global to semantic to component-specific values.

This token architecture means theming is effortless. Swapping the entire visual identity requires changing a single token file, not hunting through 200 components.

Philosophy

Systems Are a UX Problem

A design system is only as good as its adoption. I treated Orion's adoption as a UX challenge - if designers don't use it, it failed regardless of how well built it is.

That meant thorough documentation that explained the "why" behind decisions, not just the "what." It meant making components so well-structured that they answered the question before it was asked. It meant making the right choice the easy choice.

Process

How I Worked

Audit- Architecture- Build- Document- Maintain

Full design audit, token architecture planning, component builds in Figma with auto-layout and variant properties, comprehensive usage documentation, and an ongoing maintenance process for extending the system as the product grows.

Outcome

What Changed

Onboarding time for new designers dropped from weeks to days. Design-to-dev handoff became significantly smoother - developers had a shared language and a single reference point. The system is now actively maintained and extended by the team, which is the real measure of success.
