WalletGyde

A personal finance app helping users track spending, set budgets, and understand their financial health. The app had strong features but terrible discoverability - users couldn't find what they needed, so they gave up.

+32% Task Completion Rate
2.4x 30-Day Retention
4 to 1 Nav Simplification
Overview

The Problem

The information architecture had grown organically over time without a clear system. Core actions were buried 4 to 5 taps deep. The onboarding never explained the app's core value - users arrived and immediately felt lost.

Nav had expanded to 7 items with no clear hierarchy. Every feature felt equally important, which meant nothing felt important.

Research

What I Found

Users weren't failing because they were bad at finance. They were failing because the app forced them to think like a product manager instead of a person. Mental models weren't matched - the structure was designed around features, not goals.

The onboarding dropped users straight into a dashboard they didn't understand, with no orientation and no quick win to create momentum.

Solution

What I Changed

I restructured the IA around user mental models - not feature lists. Nav simplified from 7 items to 4, organized around the three things users actually want: see what I have, see what I spent, and plan ahead.

I redesigned onboarding to front-load the "aha moment" - seeing your complete financial picture for the first time. The goal was to make users feel in control within 90 seconds of opening the app.

Process

How I Worked

Research- Define- Design- Test- Ship

User interviews, card sorting for IA validation, competitive analysis, then iterative design with moderated usability testing at each major milestone.

Outcome

What Changed

Task completion rate increased by 32%. Users who completed the new onboarding were 2.4x more likely to still be active after 30 days. The redesign validated that simplifying complexity is harder than adding features - and far more valuable.
